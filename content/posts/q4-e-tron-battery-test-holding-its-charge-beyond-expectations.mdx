<p>I own a 2023 <a href="https://www.audiusa.com/en/models/q4/q4-e-tron/2025/overview/">Audi Q4 e-tron </a>and I have been very happy with it overall.  The battery range could be better but it meets my current needs.  I was headed out a two-month long trip and I was concerned that the battery on my Audi would be drained by the time I got back.</p>

<p>Some background information.  I live in the New York and I garage my car.  I was going to be out of town from January 1<sup>st</sup> through February 28<sup>th</sup>.  As you would expect the temperate in New York during these months can range between 15 degrees and 55 degrees. </p>

<p>My expectation was that the Audi’s battery would lose between 20% and 30% of its charge due to be unused and the weather having a negative impact.  To offset this expected drainage, I charged my car to 87%.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="https://david.travel/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/20250228_174258-1024x498.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-6386"/></figure>

<p>When I got home, I was super surprised that my car was still charged to 87%.  This means that it did not lose any charge in the 2 months that I was gone.  As I drove my car around, it maintained its normal charge and range.  I still cannot believe this.  My friend who has a Tesla says that his car loses a few percentage points each day.</p>

<p>How is this possible?  Any why does Audi hold its charge while Tesla does not.</p>
