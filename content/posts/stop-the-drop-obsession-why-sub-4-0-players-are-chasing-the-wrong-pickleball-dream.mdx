<p>We’ve all seen it. You’re at the local courts, watching a 3.5-level match, and everyone is trying to channel their inner Ben Johns by obsessing over the Holy Grail of pickleball: The Third Shot Drop.</p>

<p>There’s just one problem.  Most players are so busy trying to master this pro-level finesse shot that they’re actually donating points to their opponents. <a href="https://www.thedinkpickleball.com/below-a-4-0-in-pickleball-focus-on-these-2-shots-instead-of-the-third-shot-drop/">According to data</a>, the Third Shot Drop isn’t the magic wand we’ve been told it is—at least not yet.  If you’re currently playing below the 4.0 level, it’s time to stop over-complicating your game. Here is why you should put the drop on the back burner and focus on the two shots that actually win matches.</p>

<p>The Cold, Hard Data</p>

<p><a href="https://www.thedinkpickleball.com/below-a-4-0-in-pickleball-focus-on-these-2-shots-instead-of-the-third-shot-drop/">Coach Roig</a> analyzed tournament footage to see how 3.5-level points are actually scored. The results were a wake-up call:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>In women’s matches, only 13% of points involved a third-shot drop.</li>

<li>In men’s matches, that number only climbed to 30%.</li>
</ul>

<p>Translation? Between 70% and 87% of points are decided by something else entirely. If you’re spending 90% of your practice time on a shot that only matters 13% of the time, you’re playing the wrong game.  Here is what you should be doing instead:</p>

<p>Master the Gateway Shot: The Deep Return of Serve</p>

<p>If the Third Shot Drop is the Golden Child, the Return of Serve is the Unsung Hero.  It is, quite literally, the most important shot in your arsenal.  A weak, short return is a gift-wrapped invitation for your opponent to crush a drive and end the point before it begins. On the flip side, nearly 40% of points in intermediate matches are gifted via missed returns or unforced errors.</p>

<p>The Strategy: Don’t try to be cute. Aim for depth and consistency. Keep your opponents pinned to the baseline. A deep, boring return is infinitely better than a flashy one that hits the tape or lands short.</p>

<p>The Stayin’ Alive Strategy: Consistency Over Flash</p>

<p>We’ve all felt the urge to go for the hero shot—that cross-court dink at a sharp angle or the line-painting drive. But at the 3.5 level, the team that wins is usually the team that simply refuses to die.  About 18% of points go to the team that just keeps the ball in play long enough for the opponent to get bored and make a mistake.</p>

<p>The Strategy:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Give the net some space: Use higher net clearance. The net is the only opponent that never misses; stop giving it free points.</li>

<li>Positioning is King: If you aren't sure where to be, stay back. Don't get caught in No Man's Land while admiring a mediocre shot.</li>

<li>Let them beat themselves: Most intermediate players lack the patience for a 15-shot rally. If you can hit 16, you win.</li>
</ul>

<p>Final Thought: Focus on What Works, Not What Glows</p>

<p>This isn’t to say you should never learn to drop. As you improve finesse becomes a requirement. But right now? The Highlight Reel drop is getting way more attention than it deserves.</p>

<p>Master your return. Stay composed in the rally. Let your opponent be the one to chase the impossible shot while you calmly collect the win.</p>
